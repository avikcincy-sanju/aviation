Live position data uses Airplanes.live with an adaptive OpenSky health-check and fallback; recent successful aircraft are also cached locally so the page can populate immediately after a reload. Both services are community-supported receiver networks with no uptime guarantee, so a quiet scope can mean no nearby receiver is reporting rather than an empty sky. If both feeds are temporarily unavailable, the page retries with progressively longer pauses while the most recent successful aircraft data remains visible. Position ages continue updating every second during an interruption: records become stale after 60 seconds, outdated after 120 seconds, and are removed after five minutes without contact. Outdated positions remain visible on their cards for context, but are excluded from the live count, closest/highest/fastest summaries, and radar dots. Aircraft trails, projected two-minute tracks, watch alerts, filters, and session analytics are calculated locally in the browser. Aircraft type, registration, and registered owner come from hexdb.io when available. Scheduled airline origin and destination come from adsbdb.com by matching the callsign; airport names, cities, countries, coordinates, and elevations are shown when supplied. Because airline flight numbers can be reused or route records can be stale, the aircraft's live position is checked against the reported great-circle route corridor. Progress and time remaining appear only when that route is geographically plausible; otherwise the route is clearly marked unverified. Any displayed progress and time remaining are straight-line estimates, not official operational ETAs. When no scheduled route exists—common for private/general-aviation tail numbers—the page loads the public OurAirports dataset and shows the nearest known airport, clearly labeled as proximity only and not a confirmed origin or destination. Airport runway and frequency details come from OurAirports. Current U.S. station observations are requested from the National Weather Service when available, with a global Open-Meteo model estimate as a fallback; model estimates are not official METARs. The map uses Leaflet and OpenStreetMap tiles. "Registered in" is the aircraft's registration country, not its departure airport. Squawk 7500/7600/7700 are universal emergency transponder codes, and "ident activated" reflects a pilot pressing the ident button at ATC's request—both shown for awareness, not diagnosis.
